# FALSE CLAIMS ACT (31 U.S.C. § 3729)

## What It Prohibits

**Knowingly presenting false/fraudulent claims for payment to the government**

### Key Violations:
1. **Inflated Pricing**: Charging more than fair market value
2. **Product Substitution**: Delivering inferior goods while billing for premium
3. **False Certification**: Claiming compliance when non-compliant
4. **Phantom Services**: Billing for services not rendered

## Legal Thresholds

**Prosecution typically requires:**
- Materiality: False claim affected government decision
- Knowledge: Defendant knew or should have known claim was false
- Damages: Government paid money it shouldn't have

**Penalties:**
- $13,946 - $27,894 per false claim (2024 rates)
- Treble damages (3x actual damages)
- Criminal prosecution possible for willful violations

## Fraud Indicators

**Price Inflation Red Flags:**
- Price >300% of comparable contracts
- Price >200% without technical justification
- Systematic overpricing across multiple contracts

**Pattern Indicators:**
- Original low bid followed by inflated amendments
- Sole-source justification for standard items
- Consistent overpricing to same agency

## Prosecuted Examples

**US v. Oracle Corp (2011)**
- Inflated pricing on government software contracts
- Claimed educational discounts weren't applied
- Settlement: $199.5M

**US v. Boeing (2006)**
- Overcharged Air Force for spare parts
- 300-600% markups on standard items
- Settlement: $615M

**US v. DynCorp (2005)**
- Inflated labor rates on defense contracts
- Billed senior rates for junior staff
- Settlement: $7M

## Application to Our Analysis

**When to flag for False Claims Act:**
- Price >3x median for SAME contract type
- Standard goods/services (not R&D or specialized)
- Pattern of overpricing (not isolated incident)
- No technical justification in contract description

**Confidence Levels:**
- High (90%+): Price >5x, standard item, pattern present
- Medium (70-90%): Price 3-5x, some context unclear
- Low (<70%): Specialized item or missing data
